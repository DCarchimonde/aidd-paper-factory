\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

In silico prediction of absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicity (ADMET) properties is now embedded in contemporary compound-prioritization workflows. Recent systems combine large public task collections, transferable molecular representations, and high-throughput web or software platforms to screen many endpoints before experimental follow-up \citep{xiong2021admetlab,wei2022interpretable,tian2022admetboost,zhang2022helix,swanson2024admetai,huang2021tdc}. At the same time, advances in message-passing networks, geometry-aware representation learning, supervised generative representations, and chemically informed pretraining have continued to improve point-prediction performance \citep{heid2024chemprop,fang2022geometry,oliveira2022grammar,kim2024quantum}. These developments increase the number of predictions that can be generated, but they do not by themselves establish when an individual prediction is sufficiently reliable for downstream use.

Reliability is difficult to infer from aggregate benchmark accuracy. Molecular data are highly structured, and evaluation results depend on how chemical series are distributed between training and test sets. A random split may retain close analogues across roles, whereas scaffold- or similarity-based splits probe different forms of extrapolation. Recent uncertainty-quantification studies have shown that both predictive performance and the ranking of uncertainty methods can change substantially with the split design, the chemical domain, and the endpoint \citep{lanini2024unique,koetter2025upgrading,tang2025polymeruq,parrondo2026shifts}. Consequently, a high random-split ROC--AUC or low regression error cannot be assumed to represent performance on structurally shifted compounds. Rigorous comparison also requires repeated, paired evaluation rather than treating model families as interchangeable statistical replicates \citep{ash2025comparison}.

Several complementary tools have been proposed to interrogate this problem. Modern uncertainty-quantification literature distinguishes uncertainty sources and emphasizes that uncertainty quality must itself be evaluated rather than inferred from predictive accuracy \citep{hullermeier2021aleatoric,gawlikowski2023survey}. Probability calibration measures agreement between predictive probabilities and empirical frequencies \citep{guo2017calibration}. Molecular uncertainty methods attempt to rank predictions by expected error, but benchmark studies show that their quality can depend on architecture, task, and evaluation design \citep{hirschfeld2020molecularuq,scalia2020gnnuq,jiang2024gnas,lanini2024unique}. Related chemical studies have also combined uncertainty with attribution or reaction-property prediction, illustrating that actionable uncertainty can be local and task specific \citep{komissarov2025explainable,liu2024reactionuq}. Applicability-domain analysis instead asks how a query molecule relates to the chemical space represented in training; recent work has demonstrated useful distance--error associations in some structure--property spaces while also emphasizing that domain definitions are representation and endpoint dependent \citep{kim2025distance,koetter2025upgrading}. These quantities are related, but molecular novelty, model uncertainty, and realized prediction error are not identical concepts.

Conformal prediction addresses another reliability question by converting model outputs into prediction sets or intervals with finite-sample marginal coverage under exchangeability. Conformal approaches have been applied to toxicity and ADMET prediction, including graph-based and shift-aware variants \citep{zhang2021toxicity,li2024conformalized,laghuvarapu2023codrug}. However, marginal validity does not imply class-conditional or subgroup validity. This distinction is particularly consequential for imbalanced binary endpoints, where an apparently nominal overall coverage can coexist with severe undercoverage of a rare class \citep{krstajic2021critical,cauchois2021knowing,ding2023classconditional,bastani2022multivalid}. Moreover, a procedure can improve coverage by returning larger or less informative sets, so validity must be interpreted jointly with efficiency and ambiguity \citep{fisch2022trading}.

Distribution shift further complicates the interpretation of conformal results. Weighted conformal prediction can adjust for covariate shift when the relevant likelihood ratio is known or estimated accurately \citep{tibshirani2019covariate}; molecular implementations such as CoDrug use unlabeled target-domain information to estimate such corrections \citep{laghuvarapu2023codrug}. Adaptive conformal methods address other nonexchangeable settings \citep{gibbs2021adaptive,angelopoulos2023pid}. These advances do not justify a blanket expectation that a more elaborate method will improve practical reliability: estimated weights can be unstable, local scale models can fail to track realized error, and formal assumptions may not match a given chemical shift.

Selective prediction provides yet another perspective by retaining only predictions judged sufficiently trustworthy. A favorable risk--coverage curve is often interpreted as evidence that an uncertainty score identifies difficult cases. In an imbalanced task, however, aggregate risk can also decrease because the rejection rule changes the class composition of the retained subset. Coverage-aware or feature-aware predictive-inference work therefore motivates reporting not only ordinary risk, but also balanced risk and class-specific retention \citep{cauchois2021knowing,fisch2022trading,teng2023feature}. Robustness results under label noise further illustrate that formal marginal coverage and the scientific meaning of the target label must be distinguished \citep{einbinder2023labelnoise}. Without composition-aware diagnostics, abstention can create an appearance of safety while preferentially removing the class of greatest scientific concern.

The literature thus contains strong individual treatments of molecular uncertainty, applicability domains, conformal prediction, and distribution shift. What remains less developed is a single confirmatory design that evaluates how these diagnostics agree or conflict across both classification and regression, under repeated label-blind chemical splits, while preserving class-conditional validity, prediction-set informativeness, domain-conditioned risk, and retained-class composition in the same evidence chain. Existing ADMET conformal studies typically emphasize prediction intervals or marginal validity, whereas molecular UQ benchmarks focus primarily on error ranking; neither perspective alone determines whether a nominally reliable output remains informative for a rare class or a shifted chemical subgroup \citep{li2024conformalized,lanini2024unique,parrondo2026shifts}.

We address this gap with a preregistered-style confirmatory chemometric evaluation across four public molecular-property endpoints: BBBP and ClinTox for binary classification, and ESOL and Lipophilicity for regression. Frozen ECFP-based model families were evaluated under repeated random, label-blind scaffold, and label-blind similarity-cluster splits with explicit training, calibration, and test roles. The analysis integrates point performance, probability calibration, continuous and threshold-sensitive applicability-domain diagnostics, marginal and Mondrian conformal prediction, an empirical density-ratio shift-weighted baseline, locally adaptive regression intervals, and retention-aware selective prediction.

The study addresses four research questions: (RQ1) how predictive performance and calibration change across random, scaffold, and similarity-cluster splits; (RQ2) whether chemical-domain distance identifies high-error or high-miscoverage compounds; (RQ3) whether marginal summaries conceal class-conditional or subgroup failure; and (RQ4) whether Mondrian, shift-weighted, or adaptive conformal procedures improve practical reliability after accounting for efficiency and informativeness. The contribution is not a new universally superior predictor. It is a reproducible diagnostic framework and a set of confirmatory comparisons showing when familiar reliability summaries agree, when they conflict, and why those conflicts matter for chemometric interpretation and ADMET benchmark design.
