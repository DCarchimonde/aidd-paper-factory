\begin{abstract}
Aggregate accuracy can conceal chemical distribution shift, subgroup failure, and uninformative uncertainty estimates in molecular-property benchmarks. We evaluated reliability across four ADMET endpoints: BBBP and ClinTox classification, and ESOL and Lipophilicity regression. Frozen ECFP-based model families were assessed under repeated random, label-blind scaffold, and similarity-cluster splits with separate training, calibration, and test roles. The audit combined point performance, probability calibration, applicability-domain diagnostics, marginal, Mondrian, shift-weighted, and adaptive conformal prediction, and retention-aware selective prediction. Random splitting generally produced the strongest performance and calibration, although shift effects were endpoint dependent. ESOL deteriorated sharply under scaffold splitting, while mean Lipophilicity $R^2$ was negative under every split. Chemical similarity ranked risk and miscoverage for BBBP and especially Lipophilicity, but was weak for ClinTox and reversed under the ESOL scaffold split. At nominal 90\% coverage, marginal and shift-weighted classification conformal methods masked severe class imbalance: ClinTox positive-class coverage was only 0.068--0.158 despite overall coverage near 0.90. Mondrian calibration restored positive coverage to approximately 0.93--0.94, but returned ambiguous two-label sets for about 71\% of samples. Shift-weighted and adaptive regression intervals showed no consistent coverage--efficiency advantage. Confidence-based rejection reduced ordinary ClinTox error partly by preferentially removing positive examples. Reliable ADMET evaluation therefore requires joint reporting of point performance, calibration, class-conditional validity, efficiency, chemical domain, and retained-class composition.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} ADMET prediction; uncertainty quantification; conformal prediction; applicability domain; chemical distribution shift
